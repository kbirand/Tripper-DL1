Tripper Hardware · e-bike edition

Tripper Puck

A matchbox-sized GPS + IMU + barometer telemetry puck that streams to the Tripper app over BLE and reads the bike's own CAN bus. Powered from the bike through a 5 V BEC — no battery, no SD card. Headless by design: the phone is its recorder, the OLED is a convenience.

Budget ≈ ₺2.700 core Size ≈ 55 × 40 × 22 mm Power bike 5 V BEC Build time 1 weekend

The concept

Why this stays small: the entire physical interface is four things.

Status
OLED
0.91" screen — clock / data / bike-CAN + all status

Everything else — live dashboard, reviewing rides, naming, exporting — already exists in the Tripper app.

Scope pivot — e-bike edition (2026-07-21, CAN added 2026-07-23) The puck targets the e-bike: powered from the bike through a 5 V BEC (no LiPo, no slide switch) and BLE-first with no microSD — the phone records through the Tripper app's recorder pipeline, so the old v2 BLE link is the product. Consequences: LiPo + SD module + slide switch + NeoPixel are gone, the auto-record state machine moves into the app, and the puck becomes a stateless sensor that boots with the bike. It gained a second input path: a listen-only SN65HVD230 CAN transceiver on D8/D9 reads the bike's own bus (battery %, pack voltage, cell balance, motor current, kickstand, ride mode) straight into the telemetry packet. Status lives entirely on the 0.91" OLED; the two buttons are D1 (screens) and D2 (marker / mount-zero). Trade-off accepted with open eyes: no BLE link = no data for that stretch — the SD "black box" is gone; re-add it later if a lost ride ever hurts. Verify the BEC holds 5 V at standstill and doesn't drop out at low load.
Design rule Resist adding screens, menus, or more buttons. Every feature that needs a UI belongs in the phone app, where it's already built and glove-tested.

The BNO055 + ESP32 I²C clock-stretch problem — read before you solder The BNO055 stretches the I²C clock in a way ESP32 I²C peripherals handle badly. It's the single most-reported gotcha with this chip. The usual escape is to run the BNO055 over UART instead. The Gravity connector is VCC/GND/SDA/SCL, I²C only — but correction (2026-07-21, verified on the physical V1.0 board): the back-side pad row does break out PS1/PS2 (plus NBOOT/RST/INT/I2C_ADDR/BL_IND — no SDA/SCL pads), so a UART fallback technically exists via the PS pads. It would strand the I²C-only BMP280 on a separate bus, and the clean 10-minute hammer test made it moot — noted here in case a future revision ever needs it.

Mitigations, in order: drop the bus to 100 kHz (Wire.setClock(100000)), raise the I²C timeout (Wire.setTimeOut()), keep SDA/SCL runs short with 4.7 kΩ pull-ups. This is workable — but prove it on the breadboard before the case is glued (build step 2).
Run it 6-axis (IMU mode), not 9-axis (NDOF) NDOF mode needs a calibrated magnetometer. On a motorcycle — steel frame, alternator, speaker magnets — that calibration is junk and yaw will wander. You don't need it: Tripper takes heading from GPS, and the reference-quaternion math makes absolute yaw irrelevant. OPERATION_MODE_IMUPLUS sidesteps the BNO055's worst weakness. The magnetometer is the one part of this board you paid for and won't use.
Antenna rule (this decides your GPS quality) Mount the GPS patch antenna at the top of the enclosure, facing the sky, at least 15 mm away from the ESP32 module — its RF noise degrades GPS reception. If the module has a u.fl connector, route the antenna to the lid.

Bike CAN bus

The bike broadcasts everything the puck's own sensors can't see — pack voltage, per-cell balance, motor current, state of charge, rider controls. Reading it costs one 3.3 V transceiver and two wires.

Never connect USB and the bike at the same time Once CANH/CANL are spliced in, the puck's ground is the bike's ground — shared through the BEC's negative rail. Attaching a mains-earthed computer ties the traction battery's negative to protective earth through your machine. Worse, the BEC drives 5 V into the XIAO's 5V (VUSB) pin; plug a USB host in on top of that and two 5 V rails collide on the same node.

Fit a 2-pin connector in the CANH/CANL run, and kill the BEC (or unplug that connector) before you attach USB to flash. Read telemetry over BLE instead of serial — the puck is a BLE device; use what it already does.
Remove the module's 120 Ω terminator before you connect it Nearly every SN65HVD230 breakout carries a terminating resistor across CANH/CANL — on the Alkatronik board it is the SMD part marked 121 sitting between the two CAN pads. ISO 11898-2 puts 120 Ω only at the two ends of a bus, and the bike is already terminated at both: measure across the pair with the bike off and you get 60 Ω. Adding a third resistor makes it 40 Ω, which degrades reception and throws errors on a bus you do not own.

Desolder it (or crush it — nothing else shares that net), then confirm the module reads open across CANH/CANL before wiring it in.

No ground wire in the CAN tap Only two wires reach the bike harness. The puck and the bike already share a ground through the BEC's negative rail, so a third wire in the CAN tap would just add a redundant loop. This is also why a laptop on mains is such a problem: it introduces a second ground path.
Listen-only, in firmware and by intent The ESP32-S3's TWAI controller runs in TWAI_MODE_LISTEN_ONLY: it never transmits and never even ACKs, so no firmware bug can disturb the bike's controller. Bring-up is non-fatal — if the transceiver is unplugged the puck still boots and streams GPS/IMU exactly as before, and the CAN block in the telemetry packet stays zeroed with its live bit clear.
The bus, once you find it 250 kbit/s, standard 11-bit IDs, 15 messages, ~92 frames/s. Signal decodes and the evidence behind each one live in tools/talaria.dbc; tools/can_sniff.py is the host-side kit that produced them. Three fields look like something they are not — 0x103[0] is state of health (pinned at 100), 0x202[6:8] is a free-running counter and not an odometer, and 0x490[0] tracks ride mode rather than temperature. No odometer was found on the bus.

Build sequence

Bench-verify every module before final soldering — debugging inside a stacked sandwich is misery.

  1. Flash-test the XIAO

    Arduino IDE → add ESP32 boards (Boards Manager URL: espressif/arduino-esp32), select XIAO_ESP32S3. Upload Blink. Confirm the serial monitor works at 115200.

  2. Breadboard the I²C sensors — this is the go/no-go gate

    Wire the 10DOF board, run an I²C scanner at 100 kHz. Expect 0x28 (BNO055) and 0x76 (BMP280). Then hammer the bus: read the BNO055 continuously for 10 minutes straight and confirm zero dropped/hung transactions.

    Do not proceed until this is clean. The clock-stretch issue above has no UART fallback on this board — if the bus won't hold steady on a breadboard, it will not hold on a vibrating handlebar, and you need to know that now, not after the case is glued.

    ✅ Passed 2026-07-21 — 59,722 reads / 0 errors / 10 min @ 100 kHz, IMUPLUS active, max transaction 2.58 ms. Sketch: firmware/i2c_gate/.

  3. Prove the fusion output

    Adafruit BNO055 library → set OPERATION_MODE_IMUPLUS (6-axis) and read getQuat(). You should see live quaternions with no calibration dance. This is the moment the project becomes real: those quaternions feed the same reference-frame math as the Tripper app — and unlike the MPU-6050's DMP, you didn't have to fight for them.

    ✅ Passed 2026-07-21 — library path up in IMUPLUS, live quaternions + linear accel on the breadboard (test sketch since folded into firmware/tripper_puck/). Bonus: MON-VER unmasked the "NEO-M8Z" GPS as genuine u-blox 8 silicon (ROM 3.01, SPG 3.01, PROTVER 18, GPS/GLO/BDS/QZSS).

  4. Get a GPS fix

    Wire GPS to UART, run a TinyGPS++ example near a window or outside — first cold fix can take 1–5 min. Then send the UBX commands to raise the rate to 5–10 Hz and baud to 115200 (one-time config, saved to the module's flash/battery-backed RAM).

    ✅ Fix acquired 2026-07-21 — NMEA + TinyGPS++ verified, MON-VER confirms genuine u-blox 8 (ROM 3.01, PROTVER 18, GPS/GLO/BDS/QZSS). Config applied & verified same day (firmware/gps_config_5hz/): CFG-RATE 200 ms ACKed, read-back confirmed, saved to BBR, measured 5.0 GGA/s at 115200. Production firmware re-applies at every boot in case the backup battery ever drains. Bench extra that got promoted: 0.91" SSD1306 OLED at 0x3C on the shared I²C bus — now integral to firmware/tripper_puck/ (clock/data screens, hold toggle, zero progress UI).

  5. Bring up the CAN bus

    Remove the SN65HVD230's onboard 121 terminator (see the Bike CAN section), wire CTX→D8 / CRX→D9, and tap CANH/CANL at the controller. Flash firmware/can_probe/ — it brings TWAI up at 250 kbit/s listen-only and reports a decoded dashboard on the OLED and over BLE (device Tripper-CAN, Nordic UART).

    Read wirelessly, not over serial — once CANH/CANL are spliced in you must never also attach a USB host (see the warning in the Bike CAN section). Zero frames on the OLED almost always means CANH/CANL are swapped: harmless, just swap them.

    ✅ Verified on the bench 2026-07-23 — Talaria bus decoded end to end: 250 kbit/s, 15 IDs, and the module reproduced the reference numbers (81 %, 64.3 V, kickstand, ride mode) captured independently with a USB-CAN dongle.

  6. Integrate the firmware

    Combine into the main sketch (architecture below). Bench-test auto-record by shaking + walking outside.

    ✅ e-bike edition firmware running (2026-07-21, CAN added 2026-07-23) — firmware/tripper_puck/: BLE service (telemetry 5 Hz / status 1 Hz / control), OLED screens (clock / data / bike-CAN, stepped by D1), marker button, 10 s mount-zero (reference quaternion, persisted to NVS, applied to display and telemetry), GPS autoconfig at boot, and listen-only CAN folded into the 70-byte telemetry packet. Phone-verified with LightBlue: connect, notify stream, control writes all working.

  7. Solder the stack

    • Shorten all wires to final lengths; silicone wire tolerates vibration best.
    • BEC +out → the XIAO 5V (VUSB) pin, BEC −out → GND. Heat-shrink everything; the BEC's input taps the bike's pack / accessory line.
    • Put a 2-pin connector in the CANH/CANL run so you can isolate the bike before ever attaching USB to flash.
    • Both buttons in the lid; the OLED window plus the SN65HVD230 on its own short pigtail.

  8. Enclosure & mounting

    • Two enclosures, split rear / handlebar: the ESP32, CAN module and sensor board ride at the rear near the controller tap; the OLED, two buttons and GPS sit at the handlebar.
    • Print a two-part case ≈ 55 × 40 × 22 mm; cutouts: USB-C (flashing), two buttons, the OLED window, and the CAN + BEC leads. Search "XIAO ESP32S3 GPS logger case" on Printables for starting points.
    • Sensor board sits on the 2 mm foam pad — isolates handlebar buzz from the accelerometer.
    • GPS antenna under a 1.5 mm lid section (plastic is RF-transparent), ESP32 at the opposite end.
    • Mount the cases rigidly to the bar/frame (the foam is inside, not outside) — same rule as the phone.

  9. Field-verify against Tripper

    Ride with the puck and the phone app recording simultaneously. Distances should agree within a few percent; the puck's 5 Hz track should look smoother in corners, and the CAN block should carry live battery %, pack voltage and ride mode. Compare in the app — the BLE stream is the only data path, there is no file to import.

No SD, no CSV, no auto-record state machine — those moved to the phone. The puck is a stateless sensor: it streams, the app records and decides when a ride is running (and tells the puck over the control characteristic, which is what flips the OLED into trip-time mode).

Buttons

Both sit on pin-change ISRs with 50 ms debounce, so presses land even while the loop is stalled. Each discriminates click from hold the same way: the ISR timestamps the press and counts a click only if the release lands inside 600 ms, so a long hold never also fires a click.

D1 (12 mm glove button) — click steps to the next OLED screen · 3 s hold toggles auto-cycling. Screens do not rotate on their own by default: on a moving bike the screen you picked should stay put.

D2 (HW-483) — click drops a marker into the telemetry packet · 10 s hold zeroes roll/pitch/yaw and saves the reference quaternion to NVS.

Status — all on the OLED

There is no status LED (the NeoPixel was dropped). Everything reads off the 0.91" screen: a fix dot (hollow → solid on lock), LINK/ADV for the BLE state, satellite bars, MARK and ZEROED splashes, and the bike-CAN screen's own no data / off indicator. During a ride (app-driven) the whole display inverts and a trip-time screen joins the rotation.

On-air format — the BLE telemetry packet

There is no file. The data leaves as a 70-byte packed little-endian sample notified at 5 Hz — GPS, quaternion, linear/​max g, marker, then the bike-CAN block (speed, rpm, power, current, pack voltage, battery %, throttle demand, cell high/low, kickstand, ride mode). The app's ExternalSensorSource feeds it into the same recorder pipeline the phone sensors use. Full offset table: see README.md → BLE protocol.

Power

No battery. The puck runs off the bike through a 5 V BEC, so the only question is whether the BEC has margin — and it has a lot.

The BEC is oversized on purpose — headroom for inrush at boot and for a phone charging off the same outlet if you ever add one. The one thing to check on your bike: that the BEC's input line stays live at standstill and doesn't cut at low load, or the puck drops out every time you stop.

The Bluetooth link to Tripper

The puck is a live sensor for the app — this is the product, not a future phase.

One BLE service (Tripper-DL1), three characteristics:

CharacteristicDirectionPayload
telemetrynotify → phone70-byte packed sample @ 5 Hz: fix, lat, lon, alt, speed, course, quat, g, baro, marker + the bike-CAN block
statusnotify → phone14 bytes @ 1 Hz: fix quality, sats, uptime, temperature, marker

App side: an ExternalSensorSource that feeds the same recorder pipeline the phone sensors use today — the dashboard, Dynamic Island, overlays, and exports all work unchanged, now with 5 Hz GPS and the bike's own battery/drivetrain data. The phone's Marker button stamps markers into both logs. Exact byte layout and control opcodes: README.md → BLE protocol.

Pitfalls — read before soldering

  • BEC vs USB — never both: the BEC feeds 5 V into VUSB. Plugging USB in while the BEC is powered collides two 5 V rails and, with the CAN tap connected, bridges the bike's ground to mains earth. Kill the BEC or unplug the CAN connector before flashing.
  • CAN terminator: remove the module's onboard 121 (120 Ω) resistor — the bike's bus is already terminated at 60 Ω and a third makes it 40 Ω. Measure open across CANH/CANL before wiring in.
  • CANH/CANL swap: harmless but silent — you just get zero frames. If the probe shows no data, swap the pair first.
  • BEC dropout at standstill: confirm the BEC's input line stays live when the bike is stopped, or the puck reboots every time you stop.
  • GPS near ESP32: keep antenna ≥ 15 mm from the module and USB cable; test fix time before gluing anything.
  • Fake u-blox: ultra-cheap marketplace "M8N" boards are often older chips reflashed — buy from drone shops; verify with u-center over serial.
  • Vibration: foam under the sensor board or your g-force column is engine buzz.
  • Waterproofing: conformal coat the boards or accept "shower-proof"; keep the USB port plugged with a rubber stopper.
